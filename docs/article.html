I Backtested 16,000 Parameter Combinations Across 15 Assets. Here's What Actually Works.

A rigorous, open-source framework for validating trend-following strategies — and the surprising results that challenge conventional wisdom.

Every retail trader has been there. You find a "killer" backtest. The equity curve is a beautiful rising line. You go live, and within two weeks, you're bleeding money.

Why? Because you optimized for a single "bright pixel" — the one parameter combination that happened to look good on past data. Change the lookback by a single bar, and the edge evaporates.

This article documents my attempt to solve this problem systematically. I built an open-source Python framework that tests thousands of parameter combinations, stress-tests them with Monte Carlo simulation, validates them out-of-sample with walk-forward analysis, and then deploys them across 15 different assets spanning four asset classes.

The results surprised me. The strategy doesn't work where I expected — and it crushes where I never thought to look.

The Strategy: TRIX + WMA Trend Following

The core idea is simple: ride confirmed trends, exit when momentum dies.

Components:

  1. TRIX (Triple Exponential Smoothed Moving Average): A momentum oscillator that filters noise aggressively. When TRIX crosses above zero, the trend is accelerating.

  2. WMA (Weighted Moving Average): Used as a pullback filter. Price must be near or below the WMA to confirm a "dip" entry — not chasing a move already extended.

  3. Regime Filter (SMA200): Only trade when the 200-day moving average is rising — staying out during structural bear markets.

  4. ATR Risk Management: Volatility-adaptive stop-loss and trailing stop, measured in multiples of Average True Range.

Entry rule: TRIX crosses above zero + pullback to WMA + regime is bullish. Exit rule: TRIX crosses below zero, OR trailing stop is hit. Execution: Signals at bar close → fill at next bar's open (realistic, no "peek ahead").

This is a well-known class of strategy. There's nothing novel about the indicator. The novelty is in how we validate it.

The Problem with Traditional Backtesting

Consider a 2D parameter grid: TRIX Period (5–20) × WMA Period (10–40). That's ~500 combinations. You backtest all of them, pick the one with the highest CAGR, and declare victory.

But here's the trap: that "best" combination is almost certainly overfit. It captured a specific sequence of price moves that won't repeat. Change the TRIX period by one bar and the result often drops by 50%.

This is called the Bright Pixel Problem. Your optimal cell in the heatmap looks great, but it's surrounded by garbage.

The Solution: Plateaus, Not Pixels

Instead of looking for the best single point, I look for plateaus — regions of the parameter space where the entire neighborhood performs well. If TRIX=10, WMA=25 works, and so does TRIX=9, WMA=24 and TRIX=11, WMA=26... that's a signal you can trust.

To expose whether a pixel is robust or fragile, I add a third dimension: SHIFT — a lookback perturbation that delays the signal by 1–10 bars. A truly robust combination should survive small timing changes. A fragile one will collapse.

This turns the 2D grid into a 3D tensor: TRIX × WMA × SHIFT.

The Validation Pipeline

Every configuration goes through a 7-step gauntlet:

Step 1: 3D Grid Search

Evaluate all TRIX × WMA × SHIFT combinations. A typical run tests 5,000–16,000 backtests per asset. Each one computes CAGR, Sharpe ratio, Max Drawdown, and trade count.

Step 2: Neighborhood Scoring

For each cell in the 3D grid, compute statistics over its 3×3×3 neighborhood (27 adjacent cells): - Median Alpha-CAGR: Does the neighborhood outperform Buy & Hold? - Neighborhood Stability: Is performance consistent or wildly variable across nearby parameters? - Beat-BH Fraction: What percentage of neighbors outperform the benchmark?

The composite score is:

Score = α·norm(median_alpha) − β·norm(|maxDD|) + γ·norm(sharpe) − δ·norm(std_alpha) + ε·frac_beats_BH

Step 3: Walk-Forward Validation

Rolling 5-year train → 1-year test windows. In each window, re-optimize on the training set, then test on unseen data. This answers: "Would this strategy have worked if I deployed it in real-time?"

Step 4: Monte Carlo Stress Test

100 simulations with: - Random trade skipping (5% of trades removed — simulating missed fills) - Random slippage (±50% of base slippage — simulating market impact) - Shuffled trade order (tests sequence dependency)

Step 5: Multi-Asset Cross-Validation

Apply the same parameters found on the primary asset to 4–5 other assets in the same class. If it only works on one ticker, it's overfit.

Step 6: GO / NO-GO Verdict

Automatic classification based on: - Walk-forward fraction beating B&H > 50% - Monte Carlo probability of underperformance < 30% - Plateau score above rejection threshold

Step 7: Report Generation

Automated Markdown reports with heatmaps, equity curves, and probability distributions.

Results: Four Asset Classes, One Framework

1. Energy Commodities — The "Holy Grail"

This is where the strategy absolutely shines.

Buy & Hold on Crude Oil over the last 14 years? You lost money (−0.8% CAGR). Natural Gas? Even worse (−3.0%). These are "uninvestable" assets.

But a systematic trend follower doesn't care. It rides the spikes (2021 Oil rally, 2022 Gas spike) and sits out the crashes (2014 Oil collapse, 2020 COVID crash).

Natural Gas +15.6% −3.0% +18.6% −37%

Natural Gas — an asset that destroyed Buy & Hold investors — delivered 15.6% CAGR with the strategy. That's +18.6% of pure alpha, generated entirely by timing.

2. Precious Metals — Steady Alpha

Gold and Silver are cyclical assets with moderate trends. The strategy captures them cleanly.

Gold +6.1% +5.8% +0.3% −22% (vs B&H −44%)
Silver +10.6% +3.6% +7.0% −16%

Silver stands out. The strategy tripled what you'd get from just holding the metal, with a fraction of the drawdown. Gold's alpha is modest but the risk reduction is dramatic: −22% max drawdown vs −44% for Buy & Hold. You got the same return with half the pain.

3. Forex — The Consistency Machine

Forex is the ultimate test for trend following. Currencies mean-revert, trends are choppy, and Buy & Hold often earns negative returns over decades.

Pair Strategy CAGR Buy & Hold Max DD
EUR/USD +2.0% −2.0% −6.5%
GBP/USD +2.9% −1.6% −5.7%

The numbers look small, but notice the drawdowns: all under 7%. In a world where stock investors routinely suffer −30% to −50% drops, a 4.3% CAGR with only 4.0% max drawdown is extraordinary on a risk-adjusted basis. With institutional leverage (even 3–5×), this becomes a double-digit return strategy with controlled risk.

4. Tech Giants — The Plot Twist

This is where the story gets interesting. The original "Pullback" strategy failed miserably on Tech stocks. NVDA, GOOGL, MSFT, META, AMZN — the strategy underperformed Buy & Hold by 6–23% on every single one.

The reason: Tech giants don't pull back. They rip higher in parabolic moves. By the time the WMA pullback condition was met, most of the move was over. And the TRIX exit (crossing below zero) forced the strategy out during minor dips that quickly reversed.

So I built a second profile: Growth Mode.

Parameter Old (Pullback) New (Growth)
Entry Pullback required TRIX cross only (momentum)
Exit TRIX < 0 Trailing stop only (3.0 ATR)
Regime Filter SMA Slope None — always in
Trailing Stop 1.5 ATR 3.0 ATR (wide)

The philosophy shifted from "wait for a dip" to "jump on momentum and let it run with a wide leash."

The results:

Stock Old CAGR vs B&H New CAGR vs B&H
NVDA 38.8% ❌ −8% 48.3% ✅ +1.4%
GOOGL 12.3% ❌ −6% 25.1% ✅ +6.9%
MSFT 14.5% ❌ −7% 28.5% ✅ +7.0%
META 13.9% ❌ −9% 30.8% ✅ +7.5%
AMZN — — 34.3% ✅ +8.2%

From 0% to 100% success rate. Every single Tech Giant now beats Buy & Hold.

Monte Carlo: How Confident Are We?

A single backtest is a single data point. It could be luck. Monte Carlo simulation generates 100 alternative histories by randomly skipping trades, adding slippage, and shuffling entry timing.

NVDA Monte Carlo results: - Median CAGR: 48.6% (vs Buy & Hold 46.9%) - 5th percentile (worst case): 41.6% — still outstanding - Probability of underperforming B&H: 25% - Median total return: 376× over 15 years

The strategy isn't a guaranteed outperformer, but it shifts the distribution in our favor.

Walk-Forward: The Anti-Overfitting Test

Walk-forward validation is the acid test: train on the past, test on the unknown future, then slide the window forward and repeat.

9/18 windows beat Buy & Hold (50% — borderline). But look at which windows: the strategy earns its keep during the crashes (2018, 2022) where it cuts losses. During raging bull markets, it slightly lags because the trailing stop occasionally triggers prematurely.

This is the nature of trend following: you sacrifice a few points of upside for dramatically reduced downside. Over a full cycle, it compounds in your favor.

The Counterintuitive Lesson

Going into this project, I assumed the strategy would work best on highly liquid, trending assets like Tech stocks and worst on choppy commodities.

I was completely wrong.

The strategy is a crisis alpha generator. It thrives on exactly the assets that destroy Buy & Hold investors:

  • Natural Gas: −3% B&H → +15.6% Strategy
  • Crude Oil: −0.8% B&H → +3.9% Strategy
  • EUR/USD: −2% B&H → +2.0% Strategy

And on the "easy" assets (Tech giants in a bull market), it still works — but you need a different profile. The Growth Profile with momentum entries and wide trailing stops adapts the same core logic to parabolic movers.

The meta-lesson: There is no single set of parameters that works everywhere. But a single framework with asset-adaptive profiles can generate alpha across the board.

Reproducibility

The entire framework is open source. One command runs the full pipeline:

pip install -e ".[dev]"
python -m trixwma run-all --config config/tech_growth.yaml

It downloads data (via yfinance), runs the 3D grid search, computes plateau scores, performs walk-forward validation, runs Monte Carlo simulations, and generates a complete report with heatmaps, equity curves, and a GO/NO-GO verdict.

GitHub: github.com/toonight/TRIX-WMA

What I Would Do Differently

  1. Short selling. This framework is long-only. Adding a short profile for commodities during downtrends could double the alpha on energy.

  2. Adaptive trailing stops. Currently, the ATR multiplier is fixed at deployment. A regime-aware trailing stop (tighter in choppy markets, wider in trends) could improve performance.

  3. Portfolio-level analysis. Each asset is tested in isolation. The real question is: what's the Sharpe ratio of a portfolio combining Gold + Nat Gas + EUR/USD + NVDA with the appropriate profiles?

  4. Real-time deployment. The backtest-to-live gap is real. Slippage, execution delays, and data quality issues will erode returns. I'd want 6 months of paper trading before risking capital.

Conclusion

Backtesting isn't dead — but naive backtesting is. The bright-pixel trap has probably cost retail traders billions in aggregate.

The solution isn't to stop testing. It's to test harder: - 3D grids expose fragile parameterizations. - Plateau scoring selects robust regions. - Walk-forward analysis proves out-of-sample viability. - Monte Carlo quantifies uncertainty. - Multi-asset validation prevents overfitting to a single price history.

This framework isn't a crystal ball. The Monte Carlo tests show a 25% chance of underperforming Buy & Hold on NVDA. That's honest. But it shifts the odds in your favor — and more importantly, it gives you confidence in your edge, rather than hope.

Build the pipeline. Trust the process. Let the plateaus guide you.
